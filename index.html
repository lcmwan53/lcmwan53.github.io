Since I joined University of Virginia in 2018, I have been using computational tools to study ethylene epoxidation, a catalytic reaction on the Ag surface. My work can be summaried as below:

Ab Initio Thermodynamics

⇙ To understand the relative stability of different structures under experimental conditions, we used density functional theory (DFT) and ab initio thermodynamics as the tools to calculate Gibbs free energy as a function of temperature and pressure.

DFT-based Vibrational Frequency & Raman Intensity Calculation

⇘ A simulated Raman intensity vs. frequency plot for a given crystal is acquired by a two-step calculation. We first use harmonic approximations to compute the vibrational frequencies (normal modes) from the Hessian matrix. The second step is to compute the polarizability change (Raman tensor) of the crystal at each frequency, with an external electric field applied to the system.

⇗ The equations governing the second step can be found here: (Liang, Qiaohao, Shyam Dwaraknath, and Kristin A. Persson. "High-throughput computation and evaluation of raman spectra." Scientific data 6.1 (2019): 1-7.)

Machine Learning on Ag-O Systems

⇙ We tried different machine learning algorithms (linear regression, Gaussian regression, and neural network) to study the atomic interactions on the catalyst surface. The current neural network model is two hidden-layers deep and shows an elegant prediction of energetics and forces within the structure. The main goal of machine learning is to enable us to run faster molecular dynamics simulations with substantially larger systems with reliable accuracy.
